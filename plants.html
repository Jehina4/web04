Endemic plants of Mauritius

Trochetia

The National Flower of Mauritius is the Trochetia boutoniana, commonly known as Boucle d’Oreille. Officially declared on 12th March 1992, this unique flower symbolizes the island’s rich biodiversity and cultural heritage. Endemic to Mauritius, its vibrant reddish-orange bell-shaped blooms and rarity reflect the nation’s unique beauty and ecological significance.

Dombeya mauritiana

Dombeya mauritiana is a fascinating plant that once thrived in Mauritius. It was thought to be extinct in the wild, which added to the urgency of conservation efforts. Recent discoveries have shown that this tree still exists, sparking hope for its preservation. This remarkable species is part of the Malvaceae family and has a unique history tied to the island’s rich biodiversity. Botanists had nearly lost hope after the last known specimen was found in 1976. The rediscovery of Dombeya mauritiana serves as a reminder of the importance of protecting endangered species and their habitats. Exploring the story of Dombeya mauritiana reveals much about the challenges faced by plants on the brink of extinction. As efforts continue to secure its survival, this tree stands as a symbol of resilience in the face of environmental change.

Elaeocarpus bojeri (Bois Dentelle)

Elaeocarpus bojeri, commonly known as Bois Dentelle, is a critically endangered flowering tree endemic to Mauritius. With only two known individuals remaining in the wild, it is recognized for its delicate white flowers and is found in the island's cloud forests, highlighting the urgent need for conservation effortsThe Bois Dentelle faces significant threats from invasive species, such as Guava and Litsea monopetala, which outcompete it for resources in its natural habitat. Its lack of commercial viability has contributed to its neglect in conservation priorities, making its survival even more precarious

Pandanus pseudomontanus (a rare screw pine)

Pandanus pseudomontanus, commonly known as a rare screw pine, is an endemic species found in the unique ecosystems of Mauritius. This plant is characterized by its distinctive spiral arrangement of long, narrow leaves that grow in a rosette pattern, giving it a striking appearance.This species provides habitat and food for various wildlife, including birds and insects, contributing to the biodiversity of Mauritius. Its unique root system helps stabilize soil and prevent erosion, particularly in coastal areas. Efforts to conserve Pandanus pseudomontanus include habitat restoration and the establishment of protected areas to ensure the survival of this rare and beautiful plant.

Native plants of Mauritius

Bois de Natte (Labourdonnaisia glauca)

Bois de Natte(Labourdonnaisia calophylloides) is an evergreen tree native to Mauritius, found mainly in upland forests. It has thick, glossy leaves and grows slowly, often reaching impressive heights. Known for its durable hardwood, it plays an important role in the island’s native forest ecosystem. Due to habitat loss, it is considered a protected species and is part of ongoing conservation efforts in Mauritius.

Dodo Tree (Tambalacoque)

It is found only on the island of Mauritius, home of the now extinct flightless dodo bird.In the 1970s there was concern that the tambalacoque tree was on the brink of extinction. There were supposedly only 13 specimens left, all estimated to be about 300 years old. The true age could not be determined because, like most tropical trees, tambalacoque has no growth rings. Scientist Stanley Temple came up with the theory that the tree relied upon the dodo to complete its life cycle. Temple thought that before the tough seeds would germinate they must first pass through the digestive system of the dodo. The idea was that the abrasion in the bird’s gizzard and the stomach acids would start to breakdown the seeds surface, allowing water to penetrate and triggering germination.

Bois d'Ébene (Diospyros tessellaria – Ebony Tree)

Bois d'Ébene, scientifically known as Diospyros tessellaria, is a renowned species of ebony tree native to Mauritius. This tree is highly valued for its dense, dark wood, which is prized in furniture making and craftsmanship due to its durability and aesthetic appeal.The ebony tree plays a crucial role in its ecosystem, providing habitat for various species of birds and insects. Its dense foliage contributes to the overall biodiversity of Mauritius, making it an important component of the island's forested areas.

Pandanus utilis (Vacoas)

Pandanus utilis, commonly known as Vacoas, is a species of screw pine native to the tropical regions of the Indian Ocean, including Mauritius. This plant is well-known for its distinctive appearance and versatile uses, making it an important part of the local culture and economy.While Pandanus utilis is not currently classified as endangered, habitat loss due to urbanization and agricultural expansion poses a threat to its natural populations. Conservation efforts focus on preserving its natural habitats and promoting sustainable harvesting practices to ensure the continued availability of this valuable resource.

Introduced, Tropical and Ornamental Plants

Bougainvillea

Bougainvillea, a genus of flowering plants, is widely celebrated in Mauritius for its stunning, vibrant bracts that come in shades of purple, pink, red, and white. These hardy, drought-resistant plants thrive in the island's tropical climate, making them a popular choice for gardens, parks, and as ornamental hedges. Bougainvillea is often seen cascading over walls and trellises, adding a splash of color to the landscape. Their ability to flourish in poor soil conditions and require minimal maintenance makes them a favorite among local gardeners and landscapers. Additionally, Bougainvillea plays a role in attracting pollinators, such as bees and butterflies, contributing to the island's rich biodiversity.

Hibiscus

Mauritius is home to several unique hibiscus species, including the rare Hibiscus fragilis, known as the mandrinette, which thrives in specific mountainous regions. Other notable species include Hibiscus genevii, once thought extinct, and Hibiscus rosa-sinensisHibiscus flowers hold cultural importance in Mauritius, often used in traditional ceremonies and as symbols of beauty and hospitality. The flowers are also used to make herbal teas and natural dyes, showcasing their versatility beyond ornamental use.

Flamboyant Tree

The flamboyant tree (“Delonix regia”), with its vibrant red, orange, and yellow blossoms, transforms Mauritius into a vivid tapestry of colors from November to January. Often referred to as the "Mauritian Christmas tree," it symbolizes joy and beauty, making it a popular sight across the island during the festive season.The flamboyant tree provides habitat and food for various bird species, including nectar-feeding birds that are attracted to its flowers. Its broad canopy offers shade and helps to reduce soil erosion, contributing to the overall health of the ecosystem.
